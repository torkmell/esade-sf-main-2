### 1. Raiffeisen Bank International AG (Financials)

**Finding status:** Active and multi-sourced. The most controversial Western bank in 2025–2026 over its continued Russia presence.

#### Source 1 – BankTrack / KSE Institute / B4Ukraine briefing paper (April 2026)
- **URL:** https://www.banktrack.org/article/raiffeisen_exit_from_russia_significantly_less_costly_than_risks_of_staying_new_briefing_paper_shows
- **The controversy / finding:** Joint briefing paper by BankTrack, the Kyiv School of Economics' KSE Institute, and the B4Ukraine coalition, released ahead of RBI's 9 April 2026 AGM. The paper financially models RBI's continued Russian exposure and concludes the bank may be exposing investors to potential losses in excess of €5.6 billion, with a 10–30% downward impact on share price modelled under continued-presence scenarios. The paper argues RBI's current exit strategy is flawed compared with alternatives that have been left unexplored — specifically a "business freeze" strategy (winding down non-essential business) and a "dropping the keys" strategy (deconsolidating and writing down the Russian subsidiary as a total loss). Outgoing CEO Johann Strobl has publicly claimed an exit is "not in [the bank's] hands"; the paper directly challenges that framing.
- **Sustainability impact / context:** RBI is the largest foreign financial institution operating in Russia and remains on Ukraine's National Agency on Corruption Prevention "International Sponsors of War" list. Its Russian subsidiary processes payments for Russian gas transported via TurkStream — Moscow's only remaining EU gas route. This creates a direct conflict with RBI's stated commitments to human rights, responsible banking, and the bank's participation in net-zero financial-sector initiatives. The bank has accumulated approximately €7 billion in profits trapped inside Russia since the invasion, which civil-society groups argue represents complicity-by-presence with the Russian war economy. Governance succession (Michael Höllerer taking over from Strobl in summer 2026) is being framed by NGOs as a moment of accountability.
- **Company defence / response:** RBI's official position, restated by outgoing CEO Strobl in early 2026, is that an exit is "not in hands" due to (a) October 2022 Russian presidential decree requiring Kremlin sign-off for any sale of foreign-owned stakes in 45 designated banks, and (b) a prior attempt to find a local buyer that was blocked by Russian authorities. Reuters reporting confirms the bank's account that Moscow blocked a buyer-led exit out of fears the sale would expose RBI to Western sanctions and disrupt the gas-payments channel. The bank emphasises that it has materially reduced loan volumes and customer deposits in Russia since 2024 and is engaged with the ECB on its wind-down. The Austrian Central Bank publicly declined to take enforcement action against RBI in early August 2025 documents.

#### Source 2 – Reuters via The Moscow Times (October 2025)
- **URL:** https://www.themoscowtimes.com/2025/10/01/austrias-raiffeisen-bank-fails-again-to-exit-russia-as-authorities-block-sale-reuters-a90683
- **Note:** Original report is just outside the strict 6-month window (1 October 2025), but it is the foundational reporting that the in-window April 2026 BankTrack paper builds on, and explains *why* RBI is still in Russia. Included for completeness; the in-window findings above are the qualifying sources.

#### Source 3 – BankTrack on RBI AGM protests (9 April 2026)
- **URL:** https://www.banktrack.org/article/raiffeisen_met_with_protests_over_war_profits_fossil_finance_and_surveillance_tech_at_annual_meeting
- **The controversy / finding:** Coordinated protest at RBI's 9 April 2026 Vienna AGM organised by Attac, BankTrack, B4Ukraine and IFOR Austria. Beyond Russia, protesters and shareholder activists raised two additional issues: (a) RBI's continued fossil-fuel exposure — €232m in investments across more than 50 funds in expanding oil, gas and coal companies, with the bank processing payments enabling roughly €7bn/year of Russian gas exports via TurkStream; (b) RBI's exposure to controversial US defence/surveillance technology firms including Palantir. Urgewald's Nicole Rath publicly demanded the bank tighten oil and gas exclusion policies for expansion-stage projects.
- **Sustainability impact / context:** Directly contradicts RBI's PRB (Principles for Responsible Banking) signatory status and its publicly stated transition financing strategy. The fossil-finance figures are sourced to Urgewald's GOGEL (Global Oil & Gas Exit List) database, a standard reference for NGO climate-finance research.
- **Company defence / response:** RBI did not issue a substantive public response specific to the AGM protests other than its standard position that it complies with all applicable law and is implementing wind-down measures. The bank emphasises its own climate transition policies but has not publicly committed to expanding fossil-fuel exclusions.

#### Source 4 – BankTrack blog on Raiffeisen sponsorships (January 2026)
- **URL:** https://www.banktrack.org/blog/strengthening_democracy_or_strengthening_the_kremlin_how_raiffeisen_uses_sponsorships_to_hide_from_accountability
- **The controversy / finding:** NGO analysis of RBI's use of "democracy" and "media independence" sponsorships in Austria and the wider region, arguing these are being used to deflect accountability for the bank's Russia presence. Specifically connects the bank's reputational management strategy to its leadership transition.
- **Sustainability impact / context:** Governance / "social licence to operate" concern — reputational laundering allegation rather than a specific legal/regulatory event.
- **Company defence / response:** RBI has not responded publicly to this specific blog piece. The bank's broader position is that its sponsorships and corporate citizenship activities are independent of its operating decisions in Russia.

### 7. Lloyds Banking Group plc (Financials)

**Finding status:** Active, two distinct tier-1 in-window items.

#### Source 1 – OFSI / Bank of Scotland Russia sanctions fine (January 2026)
- **URL (regulator):** https://ofsi.blog.gov.uk/2026/02/23/sanctions-compliance-in-practice-lessons-from-ofsis-160000-bank-of-scotland-penalty/
- **URL (City AM, 26 January 2026):** https://www.cityam.com/lloyds-subsidiary-fined-160000-for-putin-ally-sanctions-breach/
- **The controversy / finding:** The UK Treasury's Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation (OFSI) imposed a £160,000 penalty on Bank of Scotland — a wholly owned subsidiary of Lloyds Banking Group — for breaching the UK's Russia financial sanctions regime. Between 8 and 24 February 2023, the bank processed 24 payments totalling £77,383 to and from a personal current account it had opened on 6 February 2023 for Dmitrii Ovsyannikov, the former Russian-installed governor of occupied Sevastopol and a UK-designated person. Ovsyannikov had used a UK passport with a transliteration variant of his name; the bank's automated sanctions screening failed to detect the match. OFSI calculated a base penalty of £320,000, reduced by 50% for voluntary disclosure. Lloyds notified OFSI of the potential breach on 10 March 2023 and disclosed formally on 16 March 2023.
- **Sustainability impact / context:** Direct hit to Lloyds' financial crime / governance pillar at a moment when UK banks are publicly emphasising sanctions compliance. The case has become OFSI's reference exemplar of how screening-system data hygiene gaps (spelling variants, transliteration, name structure) create sanctions risk — meaning the fine has outsized reputational footprint beyond its monetary value. Bank of Scotland reportedly handles roughly 20–25% of UK retail banking volume, so the case is being read as systemic rather than isolated.
- **Company defence / response:** A Lloyds Banking Group spokesperson stated the group takes its regulatory responsibilities "extremely seriously", that it "acted swiftly and transparently, proactively referring this one-off, isolated matter" to OFSI, and that it has "further strengthened controls to ensure we continue to meet the highest standards of risk management and governance." The bank received the full 50% disclosure discount, indicating OFSI accepted the voluntary-cooperation framing.

#### Source 2 – Motor finance class action (March 2026)
- **URL:** https://dazzlingdawn.com/2026/3/28/lloyds-banking-group-faces-ps66m-lawsuit-as-thousands-reject-fca-car-loan-compensation-scheme
- **The controversy / finding:** Lloyds is facing a £66 million class action lawsuit from Courmacs Legal on behalf of thousands of borrowers who have rejected the FCA's proposed motor-finance compensation scheme. The FCA's industry-wide remediation framework, valued at roughly £11bn aggregate, would deliver an average individual payout of about £700 — which claimants and consumer advocates argue is well below the damages they suffered from undisclosed dealer commissions on car loans. UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves has publicly cautioned courts against awarding large sums on financial-stability grounds, which critics frame as undue political pressure on consumer-protection outcomes.
- **Sustainability impact / context:** Social / conduct-risk concern affecting the bank's customer-treatment narrative. Lloyds is one of the UK's largest motor-finance lenders via Black Horse, and is therefore one of the lenders most exposed to whichever way the scheme resolves. The dispute also raises governance questions about lender influence on the compensation framework.
- **Company defence / response:** Lloyds has accepted the principle of the FCA scheme and made provisions for it in its accounts (reflected in 2025 results commentary). The bank has not commented publicly on the Courmacs class action specifically. Its broader position is that it engages constructively with FCA-led remediation processes.

#### Context (just outside window, included for continuity)
- The FCA's separate £3.97m fine on Lloyds for unfair treatment of struggling mortgage and loan customers (announced September 2025) sits just outside the window but was the immediate predecessor event. Referenced for thematic context only.

### 22. AstraZeneca PLC (Health Care)

**Finding status:** Active and material — formal indictment of former China head, with multiple tier-1 sources.

- **URL (Bloomberg, 11 Feb 2026):** https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/astra-s-ex-chief-in-china-formally-charged-with-insurance-fraud
- **URL (SCMP, 13 Feb 2026):** https://www.scmp.com/economy/china-economy/article/3343522/china-charges-former-astrazeneca-executive-what-it-means-global-pharmaceutical-firms
- **URL (FiercePharma, 11 Feb 2026):** https://www.fiercepharma.com/pharma/china-indicts-astrazeneca-and-former-exec-leon-wang-over-data-trade-charges
- **URL (Caixin, 12 Feb 2026):** https://www.caixinglobal.com/2026-02-12/china-indicts-former-astrazeneca-executive-on-fraud-and-smuggling-charges-102413981.html
- **The controversy / finding:** Chinese prosecutors in Shenzhen have formally indicted (a) Leon Wang, AstraZeneca's former Executive Vice President and head of its China business; (b) a second unnamed former senior employee; and (c) AstraZeneca's China subsidiary itself. The charges, filed November 2025 and confirmed in AstraZeneca's 10 February 2026 full-year results, fall into three categories: **unlawful collection of personal information**, **illegal trade** (relating to the alleged unauthorised importation of the cancer drug Imjudo, which is approved in other markets but not in China; investigations have also looked at Imfinzi and Enhertu), and **medical-insurance fraud** (charged against the two individuals but not against the company). The medical-insurance fraud strand reportedly involves over 100 AstraZeneca employees allegedly altering lung-cancer patients' genetic test results to make them eligible for reimbursement of AstraZeneca drugs including Tagrisso. Wang was originally detained in 2024; the November 2025 indictment is the formal escalation of that investigation.
- **Sustainability impact / context:** Material governance and S-pillar issue. China is AstraZeneca's second-largest market ($6.65bn revenue in 2025) and 2025 China growth was only 4% versus 9% group-wide, making China the slowest-growing major market. The personal-information charge cuts directly across the company's data-privacy and patient-rights commitments. The medical-insurance fraud allegations against employees — if proven — go to ethics-and-compliance culture at scale. Commentators (SCMP) have compared the case to the 2013 GlaxoSmithKline bribery scandal in China for its strategic implications for multinational pharma in the market. AstraZeneca has prepaid the contested ~$3.5m in import taxes to Chinese customs and has flagged potential additional fines of up to ~$17.5m (5x the unpaid-tax figure under the illegal-trade allegation).
- **Company defence / response:** AstraZeneca's official position, disclosed in its 2025 annual report and to Reuters / Bloomberg / FT / SCMP: (a) the company is not alleged to have received any illegal gain from Wang's offences; (b) it is *not* facing the medical-insurance fraud charge itself, only the two individual defendants are; (c) it has voluntarily prepaid the disputed import taxes; (d) the case has been consolidated into one proceeding with no trial date set. Strategically, the company has separately announced an investment pledge of over RMB 100 billion in China by 2030, signalling commitment to the market alongside cooperation with prosecutors. CEO commentary has emphasised the company's continued strategic R&D footprint (Beijing and Shanghai global hubs) and the localisation/de-risking moves it is making.

- **URL (Bloomberg, 21 April 2026):** https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/inditex-workers-press-ortega-to-halt-pact-cutting-local-wages
- **URL (Fashion Network mirror):** https://ww.fashionnetwork.com/news/Zara-workers-urge-fashion-chain-to-stop-plan-cutting-local-wages,1825629.html
- **The controversy / finding:** Inditex shop workers in A Coruña — the birthplace of Zara and Inditex's home city — have written a public letter to Non-Executive Chair Marta Ortega urging the company to halt a proposed labour agreement that they say would cut pay and benefits for local workers. The agreement, known as ARTE, is framed by the company as part of an industry-wide push to standardise working conditions across Spain. Workers argue the new framework would erase locally negotiated terms accumulated over years and would specifically reduce the premium pay structure currently in place in A Coruña, where Inditex employees historically earn more than peers in other Spanish regions. - **Sustainability impact / context:** Direct conflict with Inditex's stated commitments to fair pay, social dialogue, and decent work — pillars the company has emphasised heavily in its public sustainability narrative and on which it has historically scored relatively well versus fast-fashion peers. The conflict is symbolically significant because it is happening *in Inditex's own home city*, weakening the company's "local roots / good local employer" positioning. Inditex's broader position, restated in its 2025 disclosures, is that ARTE is an industry-standardisation initiative intended to harmonise working conditions across Spain and is being developed through formal labour-relations channels.

**Context (not in-window):** Inditex remains an active subject of the ongoing French judicial investigation into alleged concealment of crimes against humanity linked to Xinjiang forced-labour sourcing (alongside Uniqlo, SMCP, Skechers). ### 30. Ipsen SA (Health Care)

**Finding status:** Active — global drug withdrawal for safety, with FDA action.

- **URL (Ipsen press release, 9 March 2026):** https://www.ipsen.com/press-release/ipsen-voluntarily-withdraws-tazverik-tazemetostat-in-follicular-lymphoma-and-epithelioid-sarcoma-3251503/
- **URL (FDA safety alert, May 2026):** https://www.fda.gov/drugs/drug-alerts-and-statements/fda-alerts-health-care-providers-and-patients-about-increased-risk-new-blood-cancers-tazverik
- **URL (BioPharma Dive, 9 March 2026):** https://www.biopharmadive.com/news/ipsen-withdraw-tavzerik-lymphoma-malignancy-epizyme/814161/
- **URL (FiercePharma, 9 March 2026):** https://www.fiercepharma.com/pharma/ipsen-backs-away-cancer-med-tazverik-after-safety-signals-emerge-lymphoma-trial
- **The controversy / finding:** On 9 March 2026, Ipsen voluntarily withdrew Tazverik® (tazemetostat) from all global markets and for all indications, effective immediately. The decision followed a recommendation from the Independent Data Monitoring Committee (IDMC) of the Phase Ib/III SYMPHONY-1 trial, which was the confirmatory study supporting Tazverik's prior accelerated approval. The IDMC identified adverse events of **secondary hematologic malignancies** — i.e. patients receiving Tazverik in combination with lenalidomide and rituximab were developing new blood cancers — and concluded that the regimen's risks may outweigh potential benefits. Tazverik had received accelerated FDA approval in 2020 for (a) adults with relapsed/refractory follicular lymphoma with an EZH2 mutation after at least two prior therapies; (b) R/R follicular lymphoma patients without satisfactory alternatives; and (c) patients aged 16+ with metastatic or locally advanced epithelioid sarcoma not eligible for complete resection. According to the FDA's subsequent safety alert, most affected trial participants had been on Tazverik for 1–3 years; the secondary malignancies began as early as 7.5 months after treatment initiation and in some cases appeared *after* discontinuation. Ipsen has discontinued all ongoing Tazverik clinical trials and expanded-access programmes globally and is transitioning SYMPHONY-1 participants to standard-of-care lenalidomide-plus-rituximab.
- **Sustainability impact / context:** Significant patient-safety and S-pillar event. Ipsen acquired Tazverik through its 2022 acquisition of Epizyme — a deal Jefferies analysts have suggested could "shake confidence in the company's business development acumen". The withdrawal calls into question both Ipsen's pipeline due-diligence processes and the broader FDA accelerated-approval pathway (which relies on post-marketing confirmatory evidence; in this case, the confirmatory evidence revealed risk rather than confirming benefit). Ipsen has stated the withdrawal is not expected to impact 2026 financial guidance — Tazverik was a relatively small revenue contributor — but the reputational and ethical weight is disproportionate to the financial hit.
- **Company defence / response:** Ipsen's head of R&D Christelle Huguet stated: "While this is an extremely disappointing outcome, the safety of patients remains our priority." The company emphasises that (a) the withdrawal was *voluntary* and proactive, triggered by the IDMC and not regulator-imposed; (b) it is working with the FDA on next steps to execute the withdrawal in an orderly way; (c) all current SYMPHONY-1 participants will be transitioned to standard of care; (d) long-term safety follow-up of trial participants will continue under the trial protocol. The FDA in turn issued a safety alert in May 2026 advising clinicians to monitor patients for delayed hematologic malignancies even after Tazverik discontinuation.

## METHODOLOGY NOTES & CAVEATS

1. **Strict window enforcement.** The brief required 15 November 2025 – 15 May 2026. Two notable events (Alfa Laval Russia sanctions conviction, ~22–27 October 2025; Norsk Hydro Dutch court ruling, 24 September 2025) fell just outside this window and have been flagged but not counted as in-window findings.

2. **"No news is good news" applied as instructed.** For mid-cap European names (Sobi, Galenica, Sweco, Orion, Addtech, Benefit Systems, AIXTRON, etc.), absence of tier-1 controversy coverage in the window is the normal state and has been recorded as Clean.

3. **Tier-1 source priority.** Where multiple sources existed, Reuters / Bloomberg / FT / WSJ / SCMP / CNBC / official regulators (FDA, OFSI, FCA, PRA) / leading NGOs (BankTrack, Business & Human Rights Resource Centre) were preferred. Where the original Reuters URL was paywalled or redirected, mirror coverage from credible secondary outlets has been cited and noted.

4. **Paywalls / link rot.** Bloomberg, FT, and SCMP links typically sit behind logins. The cited URLs are stable as of compilation but may degrade over time.

5. **Sector context where relevant.** For Moncler, Inditex, and other luxury / apparel names, the broader 2025 Italian judicial-administration wave affecting Loro Piana, Valentino, Dior, Armani, and Tod's is noted as sector context but is not a Moncler-specific finding — Moncler was not directly placed under judicial administration in the publicly reported cases.

6. **Not in scope, by your instruction:** company-published annual reports, company-published sustainability reports, company press releases (except where used to quote the company's defence in response to a separate NGO/regulator/news finding).
