M-01
Capital Efficiency

ROIC – WACC Spread
(Economic Profit Spread)

"Is management generating returns that exceed the true cost of the capital entrusted to them?"

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

The ROIC–WACC Spread — sometimes referred to as the Economic Value Added (EVA) margin — measures the percentage-point differential between the rate of return a company generates on its invested capital and the blended cost of that capital. A positive and widening spread is the single most reliable indicator of durable economic value creation; a negative or shrinking spread signals that the business is consuming capital rather than compounding it.

Core Formulas

ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital NOPAT = EBIT × (1 – Effective Tax Rate) // Net Operating Profit After Tax; use normalised EBIT (strip one-offs) Invested Capital = Total Equity + Total Financial Debt - Cash & Cash Equivalents + Operating Lease Liabilities (IFRS 16 adjusted) // Represents all capital providers' claims, net of excess cash WACC = [Kd × (1 – t) × (D/V)] + [Ke × (E/V)] // Kd = pre-tax cost of debt; Ke = cost of equity (CAPM); // D/V and E/V = debt and equity as % of total firm value Economic Profit Spread = ROIC – WACC

For implementation, use a 5-year median ROIC rather than the trailing twelve-month figure to suppress cyclical noise. The WACC should be computed using the STOXX Europe 600 sector beta, a risk-free rate benchmarked to the 10-year German Bund, and a long-run equity risk premium of approximately 5–5.5% for European developed markets. A single-year ROIC spike due to an asset sale or restructuring gain must be normalised out.

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

This metric operates on a dual-gate architecture: an absolute hard floor combined with a relative percentile rank. The hard floor enforces the economic principle that a company failing to cover its cost of capital is, by definition, destroying institutional wealth. The percentile rank then differentiates between survivors.

Hard-Floor Gate (Absolute)

5-year median ROIC–WACC spread > 0%
Minimum ROIC (sector-adjusted) > 8%
Years of negative spread permitted ≤ 1 of 5

Hard-floor failure = automatic exclusion from universe, regardless of composite score.

Relative Scoring Gate (Percentile)

Minimum percentile within universe ≥ 40th
Score for top-quartile spread (>+5%) 100 pts
Score for spread > 0% but < +2% 40–60 pts

Score linearly interpolated between universe min and max, capped at 100.

A critical nuance: for capital-heavy sectors (Utilities, Telecom), the WACC denominator is inherently higher and structural returns lower. The filter therefore applies a sector-relative ROIC floor (ROIC vs. the sector median WACC) to avoid systematically excluding regulated industries that may otherwise suit a pension mandate.

3 — Weighting Recommendation

25% ROIC–WACC spread receives the highest individual weight because it is the most comprehensive single-number summary of capital stewardship. Every other metric (cash conversion, leverage, margin stability) is ultimately a proxy for or a consequence of sustained economic profit generation. For a pension fund whose obligation is long-duration and liability-matched, owning companies that consistently earn above their cost of capital is not a preference — it is the structural precondition for real return delivery without excessive risk. An additional 5% premium over the cash metric reflects the fact that ROIC integrates both balance sheet utilisation and income statement efficiency simultaneously.

M-02
Cash Generation Quality

Free Cash Flow Conversion Rate
(FCF / EBITDA Paired with FCF / Net Income)

"Does reported profit translate into actual cash — or does it disappear into working capital and accounting artifice?"

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

The FCF Conversion Rate is a dual-ratio signal designed to detect companies whose earnings appear solid on an accrual basis but whose actual cash generation is structurally impaired. It is one of the most powerful cross-sector screens available because cash flow is virtually impossible to fabricate over multiple years in the way that earnings can be managed through accrual choices.

We employ a paired approach: the FCF/EBITDA ratio controls for capital intensity differences across sectors, while FCF/Net Income flags the degree to which accounting earnings diverge from economic reality at the bottom line.

Core Formulas — Paired Signal

FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Maintenance CapEx // Note: use Maintenance CapEx, NOT total CapEx // Growth CapEx should be stripped to isolate structural cash generation // Proxy: Maintenance CapEx ≈ D&A for most mature industrials; // for capital-light sectors, Operating CF – CapEx is sufficient Signal A — Cash Conversion (vs. Operating Profit): FCF / EBITDA = Free Cash Flow / EBITDA // Reveals: working capital drag, lease-adjusted capex burden, // and taxation efficiency in cash terms Signal B — Quality of Net Income: FCF / Net Income = Free Cash Flow / Reported Net Income // Reveals: accrual manipulation, non-cash income inflation // A ratio <75% signals net income is materially accrual-inflated Composite Signal: 5-year median of BOTH ratios

An important implementation detail: CapEx normalisation. Some companies exercise aggressive capital expenditure in years 1–3 of a cycle and then harvest cash in years 4–5, creating a misleading conversion spike. The filter must apply a 5-year rolling average of CapEx to smooth the denominator, rather than using the single-year figure. This prevents capital-cycle timing games from inflating or deflating the score artificially.

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

Hard-Floor Gate (Signal A — FCF/EBITDA)

5-year median FCF/EBITDA ≥ 45%
Minimum annual FCF/EBITDA ≥ 25%
Years below 30% threshold ≤ 1 of 5

Hard-Floor Gate (Signal B — FCF/Net Income)

5-year median FCF/Net Income ≥ 80%
Minimum single-year ratio ≥ 50%
Interpretation below 80% Accrual flag raised

Both gates must be passed simultaneously. A company can score in the top decile on Signal A but be hard-excluded if Signal B reveals persistent income inflation. The relative scoring then percentile-ranks companies on a composite of both signals, with Signal A receiving 60% weight and Signal B receiving 40% weight within this metric's sub-score.

For Financial sector companies (banks, insurers) where EBITDA is not a meaningful construct, substitute the FCF proxy with: Cash Return on Equity = Dividends Paid + Buybacks / Average Shareholders' Equity, with an adjusted net income quality ratio using operating cash flow to net income.

3 — Weighting Recommendation

25% Equal weight to ROIC–WACC spread because cash generation quality is the lifeblood of a pension mandate. The fund's ability to distribute benefits to beneficiaries and meet liability payments depends on investee companies generating actual cash — not accounting profit. The dual-signal architecture makes this metric uniquely resistant to earnings management. Moreover, cash-generative companies preserve optionality: they can reinvest at attractive rates, fund dividends without balance sheet stress, or survive downturns without distress financing. The 25% weight reflects this irreducibility — no level of brand strength, market position, or ROIC can compensate for structural FCF impairment over a multi-decade holding period.

M-03
Balance Sheet Stability

Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio
(FCCR) with Net Debt/EBITDA Overlay

"Can the company service all its fixed obligations — in cash — without distress, even in a stress scenario?"

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

We deliberately choose the Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio (FCCR) over the more commonly used Interest Coverage Ratio (EBIT/Interest) because it is structurally more conservative and more complete. The FCCR accounts for all mandatory cash outflows — including lease obligations (which have become substantial following IFRS 16 adoption) and any scheduled debt principal repayments — not merely interest payments. A company with strong EBIT interest cover but crushing lease obligations and imminent debt maturities could still face severe liquidity stress.

The Net Debt/EBITDA overlay serves as the solvency anchor: it anchors the stock of leverage, while FCCR evaluates the flow of coverage. Both dimensions must be healthy simultaneously.

Core Formulas — Dual-Dimension Solvency Signal

FCCR = (EBITDA − Maintenance CapEx − Cash Taxes) / (Interest Expense + Lease Payments + Scheduled Principal) // Uses cash-based numerator: EBITDA less unavoidable cash outflows // Denominator includes ALL contractual fixed charges in the year // Note: this is more conservative than standard EBIT / Interest alone Net Debt / EBITDA = (Total Financial Debt − Cash & Equivalents) / EBITDA // Use gross financial debt, including IFRS 16 lease liabilities // EBITDA normalised for non-recurring items over trailing 12 months // Stress-tested using a −20% EBITDA scenario to simulate a downturn Composite Solvency Score = Percentile(FCCR) × 60% + Percentile(−Net Debt/EBITDA) × 40% // Higher FCCR is better; lower leverage is better (hence inverted)

An important advanced refinement: the filter should also compute a debt maturity profile score. Companies with >30% of their gross debt maturing within 24 months are exposed to refinancing risk — a structural vulnerability for a long-term holder in environments of rising credit spreads. A high FCCR today does not protect against a liquidity cliff caused by a poorly structured maturity ladder. This can be implemented as a binary flag (penalty applied if >30% near-term maturity concentration exists).

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

Hard-Floor Gate (FCCR)

Trailing FCCR (base case) ≥ 2.5×
FCCR under −20% EBITDA stress ≥ 1.5×
5yr minimum annual FCCR ≥ 1.75×

Hard-Floor Gate (Net Debt / EBITDA)

Max Net Debt/EBITDA (general) ≤ 3.0×
Max for regulated Utilities/Infra ≤ 5.0×
Maturity concentration flag <30% in 24 months

The sector carve-out for Utilities and regulated Infrastructure (maximum 5.0× Net Debt/EBITDA) reflects the reality that these businesses operate under contractual revenue frameworks with sovereign counterparties, making their cash flow profile genuinely more defensive and their leverage capacity structurally higher than that of cyclical industrials. The FCCR floor of 2.5× remains uniformly applied.

The stress-test is mandatory. The filter must run the −20% EBITDA scenario for every company, as this simulates a mild European recessionary environment — precisely the scenario a pension fund must hedge against. Companies that only barely cover their fixed charges in the base case represent hidden risks that simple leverage ratios cannot surface.

3 — Weighting Recommendation

20% A 20% weight reflects the critical importance of solvency without over-penalising structurally leveraged but cash-safe businesses (such as regulated utilities or infrastructure operators) that may be ideal inflation-protected assets for a pension mandate. The slight discount versus ROIC and FCF metrics acknowledges that moderate leverage in a low-cost, long-duration debt structure can be rational — the FCCR framework captures this nuance, unlike blunt leverage ratios. However, solvency is existential: any company that breaches its hard floor is immediately excluded regardless of composite rank, preserving the metric's role as the portfolio's structural safety net.

M-04
Earnings Quality

Sloan Accruals Ratio
(Balance Sheet Method — Normalised)

"Are profits driven by real economic activity — or are they being manufactured through the expansion of accrual-based balance sheet items?"

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

The Sloan Accruals Ratio, first formalised by Richard Sloan in his landmark 1996 paper, is the most academically validated and institutionally applied measure of earnings quality in quantitative finance. It is grounded in the observation that the accrual component of earnings — the portion not supported by cash flow — is far less persistent than the cash component. High accruals predict future earnings disappointment with remarkable reliability, because they typically represent either aggressive revenue recognition, understated expense accruals, or working capital inflation that must eventually reverse.

We implement the Balance Sheet Method, which is more robust to cash flow statement restatements and works cross-sectorally, while adding a 5-year normalisation to detect structural accrual accumulation rather than transient one-year movements.

Core Formula — Balance Sheet Method (Sloan 1996, Refined)

Net Operating Assets (NOA) = (Total Assets − Cash & Equivalents) − (Total Liabilities − Short-term Financial Debt − Long-term Financial Debt) // NOA = Total operating assets net of operating liabilities // Excludes purely financial items (debt, cash) to isolate operating accruals Accruals Ratio (t) = (NOA_t − NOA_t-1) / ((NOA_t + NOA_t-1) / 2) // The denominator average scaling prevents size-bias across // large and small companies in the STOXX 600 universe 5-Year Structural Accrual Accumulation = Average(Accruals Ratio, t-4 to t) // A single-year spike may be strategic investment; a persistent // positive accruals ratio over 5 years signals structural earnings inflation Quality Flag: raised if 5yr average Accruals Ratio > 8% AND single-year spike in most recent period > 15%

A high positive Sloan Accruals Ratio means the balance sheet is expanding faster than cash generation — assets are accumulating (receivables, inventory, capitalised costs) or liabilities are deflating (deferred revenues being recognised early) in ways that flatter reported earnings. A consistently negative or near-zero ratio is the hallmark of a business generating profits that are entirely real and cash-backed, with disciplined working capital management.

Important adjustment for M&A-active companies: Acquisitions mechanically inflate NOA through goodwill and acquired assets. The filter must strip goodwill and acquisition-related intangibles from Total Assets before computing NOA, to ensure the ratio captures organic operating accrual quality rather than balance sheet expansion driven by inorganic strategy.

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

Unlike ROIC or FCF, the Sloan Accruals Ratio operates primarily as a relative percentile rank with a secondary hard exclusion zone for extreme cases. This is because a mildly positive accruals ratio can be entirely benign (e.g., a company investing in receivables to support legitimate revenue growth). The hard exclusion is reserved for structurally inflated accruals.

Relative Scoring (Primary Method)

Score = 100 pts if 5yr avg < −2%
Score = 50 pts if 5yr avg ≈ 2–5%
Score = 0 pts if 5yr avg > 8%

Hard Exclusion Zone (Extreme Cases)

Hard exclude if 5yr avg > 12%
Hard exclude if single-year > 20% in 2 of 5 yrs
Cross-check against FCF/Net Income (M-02)

The Sloan Ratio is most powerful when used as a cross-validation tool in conjunction with M-02 (FCF Conversion). A company that shows a high accruals ratio AND a low FCF/Net Income ratio is sending a double-distress signal: earnings are likely being manufactured and cash conversion is poor. This combination should trigger an automatic analytical review and potential exclusion even if neither metric individually hits the hard floor.

3 — Weighting Recommendation

15% A 15% weight reflects the Sloan Ratio's role as a precision scalpel rather than a primary filter. Its function is to detect and eliminate the subset of companies that look deceptively strong on ROIC and EBITDA metrics because their earnings are partially fictitious. The 15% weight is calibrated to be sufficient to penalise high-accrual companies meaningfully — enough to push them out of the top-quartile composite score — while not overwhelming the more fundamental cash flow and capital efficiency signals. Academically, the Sloan effect is well-documented but its predictive power concentrates in extremes; the weighting reflects this asymmetry.

M-05
Margin Resilience

EBITDA Margin Coefficient of Variation
(CV) with Absolute Floor Anchor

"Across an economic cycle, how stable and structurally protected are operating margins — and do they meet a minimum threshold of structural profitability?"

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

The Coefficient of Variation (CV) of EBITDA Margin is a sophisticated statistical signal that measures the relative dispersion of a company's operating margin over a full economic cycle — typically 7 years to capture at least one recessionary episode. Unlike a simple average margin, which can conceal violent year-to-year swings, the CV rewards structural stability and penalises volatility regardless of the level of profitability. It is particularly well-suited for cross-sector application because it normalises dispersion against the mean, making a 15% EBITDA margin business directly comparable to a 35% margin business in terms of stability characteristics.

Critically, the CV alone is insufficient — a company with a consistently tiny but stable margin would score well on CV while offering no structural protection. We therefore anchor it with a sector-relative absolute margin floor and a margin trend test to detect structural deterioration that averages might mask.

Core Formula — Margin Stability Signal

EBITDA Margin_t = EBITDA_t / Revenue_t (for each year t) Coefficient of Variation (CV) = σ(EBITDA Margin) / μ(EBITDA Margin) // σ = standard deviation of annual EBITDA margins, years t-6 to t // μ = mean EBITDA margin over same 7-year window // Lower CV = higher margin stability = higher score Absolute Margin Floor (sector-adjusted): General floor (all sectors) ≥ 12% Sector floor: Industrials ≥ 10% Sector floor: Consumer Staples ≥ 15% Sector floor: Healthcare ≥ 18% Sector floor: Technology ≥ 20% // Floor benchmarked at bottom quartile of respective STOXX sector Margin Trend Test (Linear Regression): Slope of EBITDA Margin vs. time > −0.5% per annum // A negative slope steeper than −0.5%/yr indicates structural // margin compression and triggers a soft flag regardless of CV score Composite Signal = Percentile(−CV) × 70% [stability] + Percentile(Avg EBITDA Margin) × 30% [level]

The 70%/30% internal split within this metric deliberately prioritises stability over level. A company with a 20% EBITDA margin that fluctuates between 5% and 35% is far more dangerous to a pension fund than one with a stable 14% margin across the cycle. Volatility of profitability directly translates into volatility of dividends, coverage ratios, and ultimately share price — all antithetical to capital preservation objectives.

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

Hard-Floor Gate (Absolute Margin)

7yr average EBITDA margin ≥ 12% (general)
Minimum single-year margin ≥ 5% (no year)
Trend test slope threshold > −0.5%/yr

Relative Scoring Gate (CV Threshold)

Score = 100 pts if CV ≤ 10%
Score = 50 pts if CV 10–20%
Score = 0 pts if CV > 30%

A CV above 30% is a strong indicator that the business model is intrinsically cyclical or subject to external price shocks that management cannot control. For a pension mandate requiring low portfolio volatility, this level of margin instability is structural — it will manifest in dividend cuts, credit deterioration, and valuation multiple compression at the worst possible moments in the cycle.

The margin trend test is a forward-looking safeguard. A business may have a 7-year average margin of 18% but if margins have declined from 24% in year 1 to 12% in year 7, the trend signal is negative and warrants flagging even if the average passes the floor. This is implemented as a simple OLS regression of EBITDA margin on time, with the slope coefficient compared to the −0.5% per annum threshold.

3 — Weighting Recommendation

15% A 15% weight reflects this metric's role as a cycle-stability validator rather than a primary value creator. It is most powerful as a complement to ROIC and FCF metrics — a company can generate strong average returns while exhibiting dangerous margin volatility that makes those returns unreliable over a pension fund's multi-decade horizon. The margin CV is particularly important in the context of an inflationary environment: companies with stable, high EBITDA margins have demonstrated the pricing power to pass through cost increases, making them the closest proxy to inflation-protected equity exposure available in a cross-sector filter. The weight remains at 15% rather than 20% because margin stability is partially captured by the 5-year ROIC and FCF metrics already in the composite.

Implementation & Rebalancing Notes

Data frequency: All metrics are computed quarterly using the most recent TTM and 5/7-year rolling windows. Hard-floor breaches trigger immediate exclusion from the eligible universe; composite rank updates feed into the next scheduled rebalancing.

Sector neutrality: Sector-adjusted floors (FCCR for Utilities, margin floors by sector) prevent systematic over- or under-representation of any single GICS sector in the final portfolio, preserving diversification within the conservative mandate.

Cross-signal correlation guard: M-02 (FCF Conversion) and M-04 (Sloan Accruals) are structurally correlated. A 20% cap is applied to the combined earnings-quality contribution (M-02 + M-04) in the composite to prevent double-penalisation of companies that fail both for the same underlying reason.

Recommended minimum universe size: After financial filtering, the eligible universe should contain at least 80–100 companies to allow for sufficient diversification in the subsequent portfolio construction phase. If the hard-floor gates reduce the universe below this threshold, the soft-floor percentile thresholds should be loosened before the absolute floors are touched.
