M-01
Capital Efficiency

ROIC – WACC Spread
(Economic Profit Spread)

"Is management generating returns that genuinely exceed the true cost of the capital entrusted to them?"

Plain-Language Explanation

For the Project Team — What This Metric Is & Why It Exists

Imagine you give a manager €100 to invest. That €100 came from somewhere — part from shareholders who expect a return on their risk, part from banks charging interest on loans. Together, the minimum return those investors expect is called the cost of capital (WACC). If the manager generates only €6/year in profit but the cost of that capital was €8/year, they are quietly destroying €2 of your wealth every single year — even though the business looks "profitable" on paper.

The ROIC–WACC Spread captures this exactly. ROIC (Return on Invested Capital) is the rate the company earns on every euro deployed. WACC is the minimum it must earn to keep investors whole. When ROIC exceeds WACC, the company is creating real economic value. When it doesn't, it is destroying it — no matter how impressive its revenue growth looks on the surface.

For a pension fund, this is the most fundamental filter of all: we are not here to own companies that look good — we are here to own companies that are genuinely compounding wealth on our beneficiaries' behalf, year after year, decade after decade.

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

The ROIC–WACC Spread measures the percentage-point differential between a company's return on invested capital and its blended cost of capital. A positive, widening spread is the most reliable indicator of durable economic value creation. A negative or narrowing spread signals capital destruction regardless of accounting profit figures.

Core Formulas

ROIC  =  NOPAT / Invested Capital

NOPAT  =  EBIT × (1 – Effective Tax Rate)
          [Use normalised EBIT: strip restructuring charges, disposal gains, one-offs]

Invested Capital  =  Total Equity
                   + Total Financial Debt (incl. IFRS 16 lease liabilities)
                   - Cash and Cash Equivalents
                   [Represents all capital providers' claims, net of excess cash]

WACC  =  [Kd × (1 – t) × D/V]  +  [Ke × E/V]
         Kd = pre-tax cost of debt (YTM on bonds or loan rate)
         Ke = cost of equity: Rf + beta × ERP
         Rf  = 10yr German Bund yield (European risk-free rate benchmark)
         ERP = 5.0–5.5% for European developed markets
         Beta = STOXX 600 sector beta, re-estimated annually (5yr monthly data)

Economic Profit Spread  =  ROIC  −  WACC
Implementation: use 5-year median ROIC to suppress cyclical noise

For capital-heavy regulated sectors (Utilities, Telecom, Infrastructure), the filter applies a sector-relative ROIC floor benchmarked against the sector's own median WACC, rather than the universal 8% threshold, to avoid excluding regulated inflation-linked assets that suit the pension liability profile.

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

Dual-gate structure: an absolute hard floor (economic principle) plus a relative percentile rank (competitive differentiation). Hard floor failures trigger automatic exclusion before the percentile score is computed.

Hard-Floor Gate (Absolute)

5yr median ROIC–WACC spread> 0%
Minimum ROIC (sector-adjusted)> 8%
Negative spread years permitted≤ 1 of 5

Relative Scoring (Percentile)

Minimum percentile in universe≥ 40th
Top-quartile spread (>+5%) score100 pts

3 — Weighting Recommendation

22%ROIC–WACC spread receives the joint-highest weight because it is the most comprehensive single-number summary of capital stewardship. Every other metric is ultimately a proxy for, or downstream consequence of, sustained positive economic profit. The 22% weight (down from 25% in the five-metric version) reflects redistribution toward M-06, which captures income reliability as a pension-specific dimension that ROIC alone cannot address. For a liability-matched mandate, owning companies that consistently earn above their cost of capital is the non-negotiable structural precondition for real return delivery over decades.

M-02
Cash Generation Quality

Free Cash Flow Conversion Rate
(FCF/EBITDA paired with FCF/Net Income)

"Does reported profit translate into actual cash — or does it disappear into working capital and accounting artifice?"

Plain-Language Explanation

For the Project Team — What This Metric Is & Why It Exists

A company can report €500 million in profit and still have zero cash in its bank. This happens because accounting profits are recorded on an "accrual" basis — revenue is counted when a sale is agreed, not when the customer actually pays. A company can record today's revenue but not collect it for 180 days, while still paying its own suppliers and staff in cash today. This gap between paper profit and real cash is where aggressive or misleading accounting most often hides.

Free Cash Flow (FCF) cuts through this entirely. It is simply: the cash physically entering the business from its operations, minus the cash spent maintaining its equipment and infrastructure. It cannot be faked — you either have the cash or you do not.

The FCF Conversion Rate asks two questions simultaneously: for every euro of EBITDA (operating profit), how many cents actually make it to the bank as FCF? And for every euro of reported net profit, how much is real cash versus accounting fiction? A company where both ratios are consistently high is generating real, bankable profits. A company where they diverge is living on paper wealth that may never materialise — and its dividend will eventually reflect that gap.

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

The FCF Conversion Rate is a dual-ratio signal. FCF/EBITDA controls for capital intensity differences across sectors; FCF/Net Income flags the degree to which accounting earnings diverge from economic reality at the bottom line. Cash flow cannot be fabricated over multiple years in the way accrual earnings can.

Core Formulas — Paired Signal

FCF  =  Operating Cash Flow  −  Maintenance CapEx
        Maintenance CapEx ≈ Depreciation and Amortisation for most mature sectors
        Apply 5-year rolling average of CapEx to prevent capital-cycle timing distortions
        Growth CapEx is stripped: we measure structural cash generation, not peak years

Signal A:  FCF / EBITDA
           Reveals working capital drag, lease-adjusted capex burden, tax cash efficiency
           Benchmark: capital-light businesses 60–80%; industrials 35–55%

Signal B:  FCF / Net Income
           Reveals accrual manipulation and non-cash income inflation
           Ratio below 75% signals net income is materially accrual-inflated
           Ratio above 100% signals cash earnings exceed reported earnings (quality signal)

Composite: 5-year median of both signals
Sub-weighting: Signal A x 60%  +  Signal B x 40%

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

Hard-Floor Gate — Signal A (FCF/EBITDA)

5yr median FCF/EBITDA≥ 45%
Minimum annual FCF/EBITDA≥ 25%
Years below 30% allowed≤ 1 of 5

Hard-Floor Gate — Signal B (FCF/Net Income)

5yr median FCF/Net Income≥ 80%
Minimum single-year ratio≥ 50%
Below 80%: accrual flag triggered→ M-04 cross-check

Both gates must be passed simultaneously. A high Signal A cannot compensate for a failing Signal B — an accrual flag from M-02 cross-referenced with M-04 (Sloan Ratio) triggers a mandatory analytical review and potential automatic exclusion.

3 — Weighting Recommendation

22%Equal weight to M-01 because cash generation quality is the lifeblood of a pension mandate. The fund's ability to meet scheduled benefit payments depends on investee companies generating actual cash — not accounting profit. The dual-signal architecture makes this metric uniquely resistant to earnings management since cash flow cannot be fabricated across multiple reporting periods. The 22% weight (from 25%) reflects redistribution toward M-06, but with a critical link: the FCF Dividend Cover sub-signal in M-06 uses the same FCF definition as M-02, meaning strong M-02 scores are a necessary — but not sufficient — condition for M-06 sustainability. The two metrics form a causal chain rather than duplicating the same signal.

M-03
Balance Sheet Structural Stability

Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio
(FCCR) with Net Debt/EBITDA Overlay

"Can the company service all its fixed obligations — in cash — without distress, even in a simulated recession scenario?"

Plain-Language Explanation

For the Project Team — What This Metric Is & Why It Exists

Every company has bills it must pay regardless of what happens to its revenues: interest on its loans, rent and lease payments on buildings and equipment, and scheduled repayments of debt principal when it matures. These are called fixed charges — they do not shrink when sales fall. A company that can pay these three to four times over from its operating cash earnings is structurally safe. A company that can only just cover them in a good year is one recession away from a crisis.

We use the Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio (FCCR) deliberately instead of the simpler "interest coverage" ratio most basic screens use. Interest coverage only counts interest payments — it completely ignores lease payments (which under post-2019 European accounting rules are legally equivalent to debt) and any principal repayments coming due. A company can look fine on interest coverage but be heading toward a liquidity cliff because €2 billion of its debt matures next year and it has no cash to repay it.

We also pair FCCR with Net Debt/EBITDA — how many years of operating profit it would take to pay off all debt. Together, FCCR checks if the company can afford its obligations today; Net Debt/EBITDA checks if the total debt mountain is sustainable long-term. We then stress-test both under a simulated −20% EBITDA scenario to see what happens in a recession — because that is the precise moment a pension fund most needs its holdings to remain solvent.

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

The FCCR is the most complete solvency metric available because it captures all contractual cash obligations, not just interest. Following IFRS 16 adoption across European listed companies, lease liabilities have become a material balance sheet item — rendering simple interest coverage ratios structurally misleading for STOXX 600 screening.

Core Formulas — Dual-Dimension Solvency Signal

FCCR  =  (EBITDA − Maintenance CapEx − Cash Taxes)
       /  (Interest Expense + Lease Payments + Scheduled Debt Principal Repayments)
         Numerator: cash available after unavoidable operating outflows
         Denominator: ALL contractual fixed charges due in the current year
         Far more conservative than EBIT / Interest Expense (standard coverage)
         Stress version: apply −20% to EBITDA numerator = mild recession simulation

Net Debt / EBITDA  =  (Total Financial Debt − Cash & Equivalents)  /  EBITDA
         Include IFRS 16 right-of-use lease liabilities in Total Financial Debt
         Normalise EBITDA for non-recurring items (trailing 12 months)
         Sector-adjusted ceiling: Utilities / Regulated Infra max = 5.0x
         General companies: max = 3.0x

Composite Solvency Score  =  Percentile(FCCR) x 60%
                           + Percentile(−Net Debt/EBITDA) x 40%
Additional binary flag: if more than 30% of gross debt matures in 24 months
→ Refinancing Risk Flag: −10 percentile points penalty applied to composite score

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

Hard-Floor Gate (FCCR)

FCCR under −20% EBITDA stress≥ 1.5×
Minimum annual FCCR (5yr)≥ 1.75×

Hard-Floor Gate (Net Debt/EBITDA)

Maximum leverage (general)≤ 3.0×
Maximum leverage (Utilities/Infra)≤ 5.0×

The Utilities sector carve-out reflects contractual revenue frameworks with governmental counterparties — their cash flows are more predictable, justifying higher leverage tolerance. The FCCR floor of 2.5× is applied universally — no sector exemption. A key inter-metric link: a company breaching the FCCR floor will, within 12–24 months, almost certainly be forced to cut its dividend — M-03 therefore functions as a temporal early-warning signal that M-06 will confirm at the income-event stage.

3 — Weighting Recommendation

18%An 18% weight (from 20%) reflects the critical importance of solvency without over-penalising structurally leveraged but cash-safe businesses such as regulated utilities. The reduction acknowledges a deliberate architectural link to M-06: a company under meaningful balance sheet stress will almost certainly be forced to cut its dividend — meaning M-03 and M-06 together form a temporal solvency-to-income-event detection system. The overall solvency information content in the composite remains fully intact through this inter-metric reinforcement.

M-04
Earnings Quality

Sloan Accruals Ratio
(Balance Sheet Method — 5-Year Normalised)

"Are profits driven by real economic activity — or are they manufactured through the quiet expansion of accrual-based balance sheet items?"

Plain-Language Explanation

For the Project Team — What This Metric Is & Why It Exists

This is the most forensic metric in the filter — the closest thing institutional investors have to an automated accounting quality detector, backed by decades of academic research proving it works. It was formalised by accounting professor Richard Sloan in 1996, and virtually every major asset manager's quantitative team now incorporates some version of it into their screening process.

The core intuition: accrual accounting lets companies decide when to record revenues and expenses. A company can record a sale before the customer pays. It can delay recognising an expense by calling it an "investment" and spreading it over many years. Every time it does this, the gap between reported profit and actual cash grows larger. That gap is the accrual component of earnings.

The Sloan Accruals Ratio measures how fast a company's operating balance sheet (its assets minus its operating liabilities) is growing relative to its size. When it grows quickly, the company is accumulating paper assets — inflated receivables, capitalised costs, growing inventory — that have been counted as profits but not yet converted into cash. Sloan's research showed these companies reliably disappoint investors in subsequent years when those paper profits fail to materialise. For a pension fund, this is a hidden time bomb: reported earnings look fine until they suddenly don't — and by then the dividend has already been cut.

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

We implement the Balance Sheet Method with a 5-year normalisation to detect structural accrual accumulation rather than transient annual movements. Goodwill and acquisition-related intangibles are stripped to prevent M&A-driven balance sheet expansion from distorting the organic accruals signal.

Core Formula — Sloan Balance Sheet Method (1996, Refined for IFRS)

Net Operating Assets (NOA)
  =  (Total Assets − Cash & Equivalents − Goodwill − Acquired Intangibles)
   − (Total Liabilities − Short-term Financial Debt − Long-term Financial Debt)
     Strips purely financial items to isolate the operating accruals component
     Stripping goodwill prevents M&A roll-ups from gaming the signal

Accruals Ratio (year t)  =  (NOA_t − NOA_t-1)  /  ((NOA_t + NOA_t-1) / 2)
     Average denominator scaling: prevents size-bias across the STOXX 600 universe
     A €500m company and a €50bn company are directly comparable on this metric

5-Year Structural Score  =  Average(Accruals Ratio, years t−4 to t)
     A single-year spike may be a legitimate investment (e.g. inventory build).
     Persistence over 5 years signals structural earnings inflation.
     Quality double-flag: raised if 5yr average > 8% AND most recent year > 15%

A high positive ratio means the operating balance sheet grows faster than cash generation — assets accumulating or liabilities deflating in ways that flatter reported profit. A consistently near-zero or negative ratio is the hallmark of a business generating earnings that are entirely real and cash-backed.

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

Relative Scoring (Primary Method)

Score = 100 pts: 5yr avg< −2%
Score = 50 pts: 5yr avg+2% to +5%
Score = 0 pts: 5yr avg> +8%

Hard Exclusion Zone (Structural)

Hard exclude: 5yr avg> +12%
Hard exclude: single-year spikes>20% in 2 of 5 yrs
Cross-check with M-02 Signal BIf both fail → review

Critical cross-signal rule: If a company shows a high Sloan Ratio (M-04 > 8%) AND low FCF/Net Income (M-02 Signal B < 80%), both metrics are flagging the same underlying problem from different angles — earnings are manufactured, not earned. This double-distress signal triggers mandatory analytical review and is grounds for exclusion even if neither metric individually breached its hard floor. Additionally, companies with persistently high accruals that are funding dividends from inflated earnings will eventually be exposed through M-06's FCF Dividend Cover test — M-04 detects the accounting inflation early; M-06 detects when it can no longer support income payments.

3 — Weighting Recommendation

13%A 13% weight (from 15%) reflects the Sloan Ratio's role as a precision forensic scalpel — its function is to catch the subset of companies that appear strong on M-01 and M-02 precisely because their earnings are partially fictitious. The weight reduction is calibrated around a key structural insight: M-04 and M-06 form a temporal arc — M-04 detects balance sheet earnings inflation in the present; M-06 detects when that inflation can no longer support income payments in the future. Together they contribute approximately 25% of composite information content, with M-04's earlier detection value reflected in its 13% individual weight.

M-05
Margin Resilience

EBITDA Margin Coefficient of Variation
(CV) with Absolute Floor Anchor & Trend Test

"Across a full economic cycle, how stable are operating margins — and are they structurally expanding or being slowly eroded?"

Plain-Language Explanation

For the Project Team — What This Metric Is & Why It Exists

Most financial screens look at a company's average profit margin and check if it's above a threshold. The problem: a company can have a perfectly adequate average margin while swinging violently between boom and bust years. A steel producer might average a 14% EBITDA margin over seven years — but if it earned 28% in peak years and 2% in trough years, its business is deeply cyclical and unreliable. A basic average margin screen would pass it; a pension fund absolutely should not hold it.

The Coefficient of Variation (CV) is a statistical measure that captures exactly this. It calculates how much annual margins fluctuate relative to their own average. A low CV means margins are stable — the company performs consistently regardless of economic conditions. A high CV means margins are volatile — the company's profitability is hostage to factors it cannot control, such as commodity prices, construction cycles, or consumer discretionary spending.

We also run a trend test using linear regression. A company might have a 7-year average margin of 18% that passes our floor — but if margins declined every year from 24% to 12%, the business is in structural decline. The average hides this. The trend line catches it. This is our forward-looking safeguard against locking the pension fund into a business that is slowly losing its competitive edge, even if it still looks adequate today. Companies with stable, high margins across cycles are demonstrating genuine pricing power — the ability to raise prices when costs rise — which is the closest thing equity investing has to inflation protection.

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

The Coefficient of Variation of EBITDA Margin measures relative margin dispersion over a 7-year window covering at least one full European recession cycle. It is dimensionless and fully cross-sector comparable because it normalises dispersion against the mean. The 70/30 internal split deliberately prioritises stability over level.

Core Formula — Margin Stability and Trend Signal

EBITDA Margin (year t)  =  EBITDA_t / Revenue_t

Coefficient of Variation  =  StdDev(Margin, 7yr)  /  Mean(Margin, 7yr)
     Lower CV = higher structural margin stability = higher composite score
     Dimensionless: allows direct cross-sector comparison (FMCG vs Industrials)

Sector-Adjusted Absolute Margin Floors:
     General (all sectors): 12%     Industrials: 10%
     Consumer Staples: 15%          Healthcare / Pharma: 18%
     Technology: 20%                Financials: sector-specific proxy
     Benchmarked at the bottom quartile of each STOXX 600 GICS sector

Margin Trend Test (OLS Linear Regression):
     Fit: EBITDA Margin  =  intercept + slope x Year  (years t-6 to t)
     Condition: slope must be greater than −0.5% per annum
     A slope steeper than −0.5%/yr = structural compression flag
     Applied even if the 7yr average passes the absolute floor

Composite Signal  =  Percentile(−CV) x 70%   [stability dominates]
                  +  Percentile(Mean Margin) x 30%   [level as secondary anchor]

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

Hard-Floor Gate (Absolute Margin)

7yr average EBITDA margin≥ 12% general
Minimum in any single year≥ 5%
OLS slope threshold> −0.5%/yr

Relative Scoring Gate (CV)

Score = 100 pts if CV≤ 10%
Score = 50 pts if CV10% – 20%
Score = 0 pts if CV> 30%

A CV above 30% indicates the business model is intrinsically cyclical — driven by commodity spreads, construction cycles, or discretionary consumer spending it cannot control. For a pension mandate, this margin instability is structural: it will manifest simultaneously as dividend cuts in trough years (M-06), leveraged balance sheets when revenues fall (M-03), and collapsed ROIC below WACC during downturns (M-01). M-05 is therefore a systemic risk detector whose consequences cascade across the entire composite.

3 — Weighting Recommendation

13%A 13% weight (from 15%) reflects this metric's role as a cycle-stability validator whose downstream consequences are now captured by dedicated metrics elsewhere. The margin CV is most valuable as a systemic risk signal — companies with high margin volatility will underperform on ROIC (M-01), FCF conversion (M-02), solvency (M-03), and dividend continuity (M-06) all simultaneously. In an inflationary environment, margin CV is also the closest quantitative proxy for structural pricing power available in a cross-sector filter — a critical characteristic for a mandate that must deliver inflation-protected real returns to beneficiaries.

M-06
Income Reliability — New Metric

Dividend Sustainability Index
(DSI — FCF Cover × Continuity × Payout Discipline)

"Can the company maintain and grow its cash distributions reliably enough to fund the pension fund's own scheduled benefit payments — across a full economic cycle and into the next?"

Plain-Language Explanation

For the Project Team — Why This Metric Was Added & What It Does

The first five metrics screen for financial quality — they ensure we only own businesses that are genuinely strong, well-run, and structurally sound. But a pension fund has one dimension that a general equity quality fund does not: it must physically pay cash to its beneficiaries on a scheduled basis. Retired teachers, nurses, and civil servants receive their pension income every month, on time. The fund cannot delay these payments because its portfolio companies had a bad quarter or decided to reinvest their cash instead of distributing it.

This means owning a company with excellent ROIC and FCF but no consistent dividend — for example, a high-growth tech company reinvesting everything into expansion — is still a poor fit for this mandate regardless of how good its other metrics are. We specifically need companies that consistently distribute cash to shareholders and have done so reliably through both good times and bad, including through the 2020 COVID shock when roughly 40% of STOXX Europe 600 companies suspended or cut their dividends.

The Dividend Sustainability Index (DSI) tests three things simultaneously: (1) Is the dividend funded by real free cash flow, not accounting profits or debt? (2) Has the company actually maintained its dividend through at least one full economic cycle — including 2020? (3) Is the payout ratio sensible — large enough to serve the income mandate, but not so large it leaves no buffer when earnings fall? All three must pass. A company that scores perfectly on two but fails one is still a structural income risk for this mandate.

1 — Metric Name & Technical Definition

The DSI is a composite of three sub-signals tested through a sequential three-layer gate structure. No single strong sub-signal can compensate for structural weakness in another — all three dimensions must pass simultaneously. The metric operates through a binary pre-screen (applied before composite scoring), hard-floor checks (within scoring), and a percentile ranking (for survivor differentiation).

Sub-Signal A — FCF Dividend Cover (Primary Gate — 50% of DSI score)

FCF Dividend Cover  =  Free Cash Flow  /  Ordinary Dividends Paid

FCF definition: identical to M-02 (Operating Cash Flow − Maintenance CapEx)
Consistency is intentional: the same FCF engine powers both the M-02 conversion
score and the dividend funding test — confirming both metrics with one data stream

Ordinary dividends ONLY. Special and one-off dividends are excluded.
Special dividends are logged as a positive qualitative flag but do NOT improve
the sustainability score — this prevents companies from masking structural dividend
weakness with one non-recurring payment, a common practice in European corporate finance.

Cover ratio of 2.0x means: for every €1 distributed, €2 of FCF is generated
Hard Floor:   5-year median FCF Dividend Cover   ≥  2.0x
Soft Floor:   Minimum single-year cover           ≥  1.2x
              Cover below 1.0x in any year = dividend funded by debt or reserves
              Two or more years below 1.0x = hard exclusion regardless of median

Sub-Signal B — Dividend Continuity Score (35% of DSI score)

Continuity Score  =  Years with Maintained or Grown DPS  /  Total Years in 10yr Window

"Maintained or grown" = dividend per share (DPS) is greater than or equal to prior year DPS
Any reduction in DPS — even a 5% cut — counts as a cut. No exceptions.
A cut of more than 20% in any single year = "hard continuity break"
Subsequent reinstatement does NOT erase the historical cut from the score.

WHY 10 YEARS: the longest lookback in this framework, by design.
The 2015–2025 window covers: European sovereign debt crisis tail (2015–16),
2018–19 global growth deceleration, 2020 COVID pandemic shock (approx. 40% of
STOXX 600 suspended or cut ordinary dividends), 2021–22 supply-chain inflation
crisis, and 2023–24 higher-for-longer rate environment.
A company maintaining or growing dividends through all of these is demonstrably
different from one that only held dividends in the easy 2021–2024 period.
A 5-year window starting in 2020 would miss the COVID shock for 2025 screening.

LAYER 1 — BINARY PRE-SCREEN (applied BEFORE composite scoring begins):
Hard Exclusion:  Dividend cuts in more than 2 of the last 10 years
This gate eliminates serial cutters entirely from the investable universe.
It is applied before M-01 through M-05 composite scores are computed.
A company with perfect M-01–M-05 scores is still excluded here if it cut
its dividend 3 or more times in the past decade.

Target Continuity Score:    0.80 or above (8 of 10 years maintained or grown)
Strong Continuity Score:    1.00 (Dividend Aristocrat — 10 of 10 years)
Progressive Policy Modifier: +5 percentile points for board-approved progressive
dividend policy with a stated minimum DPS growth floor (e.g., inflation + 0–2%/yr)

Sub-Signal C — Payout Discipline Ratio (15% of DSI score)

Net Payout Ratio  =  (Ordinary Dividends Paid + Share Buybacks)
                  /  Free Cash Flow   [both as 5-year averages]

Buybacks included to prevent gaming: companies substituting buybacks for dividends
to make payout look low while total capital return is actually high are captured.
Both forms of return compete for the same FCF and both create future expectations.

Target Range:    40% to 75%   Adequate distribution + retained FCF buffer
Hard Ceiling:    above 90%    Nearly all FCF returned — dividend at structural risk
                              Any earnings shortfall forces a cut or requires borrowing
Floor Flag:      below 15%    Capital allocation misalignment — mandatory analyst review
                              Company hoards cash and does not serve the income mandate
                              Not excluded, but flagged before any portfolio inclusion

DSI Composite Score  =  Percentile(FCF Cover)        x 50%
                      + Percentile(Continuity Score)   x 35%
                      + Percentile(Payout Discipline)  x 15%

FCF Cover dominates at 50%: the only mathematically rigorous test of whether
dividends are genuinely cash-funded vs. accounting-funded.
Continuity Score at 35%: historical evidence of income reliability through cycles.
Payout Discipline at 15%: structural headroom — confirms long-run viability of policy.
Sector Substitutions: For banks and insurers, replace FCF Dividend Cover with: Dividends / (Net Income − Regulatory Capital Deductions). For REITs and infrastructure companies: use Dividends / AFFO (Adjusted Funds From Operations) — the sector-standard proxy for distributable cash. The 10-year continuity window and the binary Layer 1 pre-screen thresholds apply identically across all sectors with no carve-outs.

2 — Filtering Logic & Application

M-06 uniquely combines three sequential layers applied in order. This architecture reflects a fundamental difference from the other five metrics: income disruption is not merely a financial loss for a pension fund — it is an operational crisis that forces the fund to liquidate assets at potentially adverse valuations to fund scheduled benefit payments.

Layer 1 — Binary Pre-Screen (Universal Gate)

Max dividend cuts in 10yr window≤ 2 cuts
Max single-year cut magnitude≤ 20%
Min consecutive dividend history≥ 7 years
Failure consequenceImmediate exclusion

Layer 2 — Hard-Floor Gates (Within DSI Scoring)

5yr median FCF Dividend Cover≥ 2.0×
Minimum single-year Cover≥ 1.2×
Net Payout Ratio ceiling≤ 90%

Layer 3 is the DSI Composite Score percentile ranking — applied only to companies that pass Layers 1 and 2. It differentiates between strong income profiles (10/10 continuity, 3.5× FCF cover, 55% payout ratio) and borderline ones (8/10 continuity, 2.1× cover, 72% payout ratio), producing the final M-06 sub-score entering the composite.

3 — Weighting Recommendation

12%A 12% weight — the lowest individual weight in the composite — is deliberate and considered, not a signal that income reliability matters least. Three structural realities justify it. First, the binary Layer 1 pre-screen means dividend reliability already acts as an absolute exclusion gate before composite scoring begins, giving M-06 disproportionate influence relative to its numerical weight alone. Second, companies genuinely strong on M-01, M-02, and M-03 will almost always have the financial capacity to sustain dividends — sustainability is partly downstream of those three metrics, so a higher weight would create meaningful double-counting. Third, 12% is precisely calibrated to produce a composite-score gap large enough to influence final universe selection — differentiating a Dividend Aristocrat with 10/10 continuity and 3.5× FCF cover from a borderline payer, without overwhelming the fundamental economic quality signals that ultimately drive long-term compounding returns.

Implementation, Sequencing & Rebalancing Notes

Execution sequence: M-06 Layer 1 binary pre-screen is run first — before any metric is computed — eliminating serial dividend cutters from the universe. Only survivors enter M-01–M-06 hard-floor checks. Only double-survivors enter composite percentile scoring.

Data frequency: M-01–M-05 recalculated quarterly using TTM and 5/7yr rolling windows. M-06 continuity score updated annually (DPS histories are annual); FCF Dividend Cover updated quarterly using the M-02 FCF definition.

Sector neutrality: Sector-adjusted floors (FCCR for Utilities, margin floors by GICS sector, AFFO substitute for REITs in M-06) prevent systematic over/under-representation of any sector. The M-06 binary pre-screen applies sector-neutrally — regulated utilities that cut dividends during rate cycles are penalised identically to cyclical industrials.

Cross-signal correlation guard: M-02 and M-04 are structurally correlated — a 20% cap applies to their combined composite contribution to prevent double-penalisation of the same underlying weakness. M-06 FCF Cover and M-02 FCF/EBITDA share the same FCF denominator — a 10% cross-correlation cap applies at the composite level.

Minimum universe size: Post-screening, the eligible universe should contain at least 80–100 companies. If hard floors reduce the universe below this threshold, loosen soft-floor percentile thresholds first. The M-06 binary pre-screen is the last gate to be relaxed — income reliability is the most structurally non-negotiable characteristic of this mandate.
